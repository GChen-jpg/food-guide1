Step-by-Step Guide
How to actually build a balanced plate

Canada's Food Guide tells you what to eat. This section teaches you how - with specific portions, real measurements, and the steps to follow every time you make a meal.

Vegetables & Fruits
Fill this first - before anything else
½
1 to 1.5 cups of cooked broccoli, carrots, or green beans
2 cups of raw leafy greens like spinach or arugula
1 medium roasted sweet potato + a handful of cherry tomatoes
Any two vegetables combined to genuinely cover half the plate
A common mistake: three cherry tomatoes or a small side salad is not half a plate. Half means half - it should be the dominant thing you see on your plate.
Protein Foods
Roughly the size of your palm
¼
75–90g cooked chicken, fish, or turkey - about the size of a deck of cards
¾ cup cooked lentils or black beans (roughly the size of a tennis ball)
2 large eggs
150g firm tofu - approximately the size of a hockey puck
¾ cup Greek yogurt (used as protein, not a side)
A standard chicken breast from most Canadian grocery stores is 200–250g - already 2–3x the Food Guide portion. Cook it, but use only part of it on your plate tonight. Use the rest tomorrow.

Tennis ball size - not the whole pot
¼

½ cup cooked whole wheat pasta
1 slice of 100% whole grain bread
½ a whole grain pita or wrap
½ cup rolled oats (dry) at breakfast
How to spot a real whole grain: flip the package and check the ingredient list. The very first ingredient must include the word "whole" - e.g., whole wheat, whole oat, whole rye. "Multigrain" or "enriched flour" does not count.

Vegetables & fruits - fill this section first. If you plate your protein first, you will unconsciously leave too little room for vegetables.
Protein - aim for plant-based at least 2–3 nights per week. Lentils, chickpeas, tofu, and edamame are cheaper and higher in fibre than most meats.
Whole grains - a ½ cup serving is smaller than most people expect. Measure it once so you know what it looks like on your plate.
Use a 9-inch plate
Most dinner plates sold in Canada are 11–12 inches. Research shows that larger plates cause people to serve themselves significantly more food without realizing it. A 9-inch plate makes the Food Guide proportions visually accurate and keeps portions realistic.
1
Choose a 9-inch plate
Before you even open the fridge, set out a standard 9-inch dinner plate. The plate size determines how much you serve yourself. If the only plates you own are larger, consider buying one or two 9-inch plates for daily use - this single change has measurable impact on portion sizes.
2
Fill half the plate with vegetables first - before anything else
This is the single most important step and the one most people skip. Do not plate your protein or grains first and try to fit vegetables around them afterward. Start with vegetables. Fill the left half of the plate completely before you touch anything else. This ensures vegetables are the main event, not the afterthought.
If fresh vegetables are not available or affordable, frozen and canned options are nutritionally equivalent and often cheaper. Rinse canned vegetables to lower the sodium content.
3
Add protein to one quarter of the plate
The top-right quarter of the plate is for protein. Place your palm flat on the counter - that palm size (not including fingers) represents roughly 75–90g, which is the appropriate serving. If you are using lentils or beans, ¾ cup fills the quarter comfortably. Aim to choose a plant-based protein at least twice this week.
4
Add whole grains to the remaining quarter
The bottom-right quarter is for whole grains. A ½ cup of cooked grain - brown rice, quinoa, or whole wheat pasta - fills this section. This portion is smaller than most people expect. Measure it at least once so you have a visual reference for future meals without needing to measure every time.
For mixed meals like stir-fries, soups, or grain bowls that do not fit neatly on a plate, apply the same proportions mentally: more vegetables by volume than anything else, a modest amount of protein, and a small base of whole grains.
5
Place a glass of water on the table before you sit down
This is a habit cue, not just a hydration tip. Placing water on the table before you eat makes it the default drink. You will reach for it without thinking. If you currently drink juice or soda with dinner, do not try to eliminate it entirely this week - just add the water alongside it and reduce the other drink gradually over two to three weeks.
6
Sit down and remove one screen before eating
You do not need to eat in silence. But eating while watching a screen consistently leads people to consume more than they intended without noticing. Remove one distraction - phone off the table, or television off - and eat with at least minimal attention on your food. This alone is the beginning of mindful eating.

When meals don't fit a plate
Soups, stir-fries, tacos, and grain bowls don't look like the plate diagram - and that's fine. Apply the proportions mentally: a vegetable soup should have more vegetables than anything else by volume. A stir-fry should be mostly vegetables, with smaller amounts of protein and grain. A grain bowl starts with greens, not grain.
1
Before you eat: pause and rate your hunger
Before picking up your fork, sit down and ask yourself: on a scale of 1 to 10, how hungry am I right now? If you are below a 4, you may be eating out of boredom, stress, habit, or because food is simply in front of you - not genuine hunger. This pause does not mean you should not eat. It simply builds awareness of why you are eating, which is the foundation of a healthier relationship with food.
2
Put your fork down between bites
This feels awkward at first. That awkwardness is intentional - it forces a physical pause between bites. Your body takes 15 to 20 minutes to signal fullness from the start of a meal. Eating quickly means you can be well past comfortable fullness before that signal arrives. Putting your fork down briefly between bites extends your eating time without requiring willpower.
You do not need to do this for every bite. Even 5–6 pauses during a meal extends the total eating time meaningfully.
3
Remove one screen from your meal - not all of them
Research consistently shows that eating while distracted by screens leads people to eat faster and consume more without noticing. You do not need to eat in complete silence. Start by removing your phone from the table at one meal per day - whichever meal is easiest. Do not attempt to change all your meals at once. One change, consistently, is more effective than several changes attempted and abandoned.
4
Aim to stop eating at a 7 on the hunger scale
Comfortably full - satisfied but not stuffed - is approximately a 7 out of 10. This is harder to identify than it sounds, especially for adults who grew up being told to finish everything on their plate. Practicing this takes several weeks before it starts to feel intuitive. A practical cue: when you notice that you have slowed down your eating pace naturally, you are likely approaching a 7.
5
After overeating: note when and why - not to feel guilty, but to find the pattern
Most overeating is not random. It tends to happen at predictable times - late at night, during stress, in social situations, or when a specific food is visible. For one week, make a quick note after any meal where you ate more than intended: the time, the situation, and your hunger level going in. Within a week you will likely see a clear pattern - and patterns are far easier to address than vague feelings of "bad eating."
This is not a food diary or calorie tracking. It is a pattern-finding exercise. One week is usually enough.

The most common mindful eating mistake
Trying to change everything at once. Pick one step from this list - just one - and practice it at every meal for two weeks before adding another. Sustainable habit change is built one step at a time, not all at once.
1
Ignore the front of the package entirely
Words like "multigrain," "made with whole grain," "hearty," "natural," or "brown" on the front of a package are marketing language and are not regulated in the same way as the ingredient list. A product can use these words even if whole grain is not the primary ingredient. The front of the package tells you nothing reliable about whether a product is truly whole grain.
2
Flip the package and find the ingredient list
The ingredient list is on the back or side of every packaged food in Canada. Ingredients are listed in order from most to least by weight. The ingredient that appears first is present in the largest amount. This is the only place that tells you what a product is actually made of.
3
Check that the very first ingredient says "whole"
For a product to be a true whole grain food, the first ingredient must include the word "whole." Look for: whole wheat flour, whole grain oats, whole grain rye, whole grain corn. If the first ingredient says "enriched flour," "wheat flour," "unbleached flour," or anything without "whole," the product is primarily made from refined grain - regardless of what the front of the package claims.
"Enriched" means nutrients were added back after being removed during processing. It does not mean the grain is whole. Enriched flour is still refined flour.
4
Check the fibre content on the Nutrition Facts table
As a general rule, a genuinely whole grain product will have at least 2 grams of dietary fibre per serving - and ideally 3 or more. Find the Nutrition Facts table (the black-and-white box) and look for "Dietary Fibre" under "Total Carbohydrate." A fibre content of less than 1g per serving is a strong signal that the product is primarily refined grain, even if it uses whole grain language.
5
Practical whole grains that require no label reading
The simplest approach is to choose grains that are inherently whole: oats, brown rice, quinoa, barley, bulgur, farro, millet. These come in their whole form with no processing and no label confusion. Oats cooked on the stovetop, brown rice instead of white, quinoa as a base for a grain bowl - these require no ingredient list checking because the food itself is the whole grain.
Frozen brown rice microwaves in 3 minutes. It is just as nutritious as stovetop, costs less per serving than most packaged grain products, and requires no label reading at all.
Reading an Ingredient List - Example
INGREDIENTS (Product A - Whole Grain):
Whole wheat flour, water, yeast, salt, sunflower oil
First ingredient says "whole" - this is a genuine whole grain product
INGREDIENTS (Product B - Not Whole Grain):
Enriched wheat flour, whole wheat flour, water, yeast, sugar, salt
First ingredient is enriched (refined) flour. The "whole wheat flour" is secondary - this is primarily a refined grain product, despite the packaging.
No-label whole grains - always safe:
Oats Brown Rice Quinoa Barley Bulgur Farro Millet
The "multigrain" trap
"Multigrain" means a product contains more than one type of grain - but none of them need to be whole. A multigrain product made from five different refined grains is still a refined grain product. Always check the ingredient list, not the front-of-package claim.

Healthy Eating Tips
Beyond what you eat

Canada's Food Guide emphasizes that healthy eating is also about how you eat - your habits, mindset, and environment matter just as much as your food choices.

Be Mindful

Eat slowly, without distractions. Pay attention to hunger and fullness cues.

Learn more ↓
Mindful eating means noticing the colours, smells, textures, and flavours of your food. Try putting your fork down between bites. Avoid eating in front of screens - it leads to consuming 15–25% more calories without realizing it.

Cook More Often

Home cooking gives you control over ingredients, portions, and nutrition.

Learn more ↓
People who cook at home more frequently eat fewer calories, less sugar, and less fat. Even simple meals matter. Batch cooking on weekends can make weeknight meals stress-free. Frozen vegetables and canned legumes are healthy shortcuts.

Enjoy Your Food

Food is about pleasure and culture, not just nutrients. Enjoy every meal.

Learn more ↓
A positive relationship with food supports both physical and mental health. Avoid guilt around eating. All foods can fit into a balanced diet - the key is overall patterns over time, not single meals. Celebrate cultural food traditions.

Eat with Others

Sharing meals strengthens social bonds and promotes healthier eating habits.

Learn more ↓
Research shows people who eat with others regularly are more likely to eat a wider variety of nutritious foods. Family meals are especially important for children, but community eating benefits adults too. Try scheduling one shared meal per day.

Limit Processed Foods

Highly processed foods are often high in sodium, sugar, and unhealthy fats.

Learn more ↓
Ultra-processed foods include packaged snacks, fast food, sugary drinks, and many breakfast cereals. They account for nearly half of Canadians' daily caloric intake. Focus on whole or minimally processed foods as the majority of your diet.

Know Food Marketing

Advertising is designed to influence your choices - often toward unhealthy options.

Learn more ↓
Health claims on packaging can be misleading. "Natural," "low fat," or "multigrain" doesn't always mean nutritious. Always check the Nutrition Facts table. Children are particularly vulnerable to food marketing -Health Canada is working to restrict it.

Total Fat Watch
Fat is essential for your body - but not all fats are equal. The total fat line includes healthy unsaturated fats (from avocado, nuts, olive oil) and less healthy saturated fats. Aim to get fats mostly from plant-based sources. 10% DV is moderate - not a concern on its own.
Saturated Fat Limit
Saturated fat is found in butter, fatty meats, and full-fat dairy. Too much can raise LDL ("bad") cholesterol.Health Canada recommends choosing foods with 5% DV or less when possible. 10% DV is getting close to the "a lot" threshold - worth noting.
Trans Fat Limit
Trans fats raise bad cholesterol AND lower good cholesterol - a double hit to heart health.Health Canada has banned artificial trans fats in food since 2018. 0g is ideal. Small amounts may still appear naturally in some dairy and meat products.
Sodium Limit
Sodium (salt) is the #1 thing to watch on Canadian nutrition labels. Most Canadians eat almost double the recommended amount. 21% DV in one serving is high - nearly a quarter of your daily max. High sodium is linked to high blood pressure and heart disease. Look for products with 5% DV or less.
Total Carbohydrate Watch
Carbs are your body's main energy source. But not all carbs are the same. This number includes fibre (good!), natural sugars, and added sugars. The key is the quality of carbs - whole grains and vegetables are far better than refined carbs and sugary snacks.
Dietary Fibre Good
Fibre is one of the most important nutrients most Canadians don't get enough of. It supports digestion, helps you feel full, lowers cholesterol, and reduces risk of diabetes and heart disease. 14% DV is great! Aim for 25–38g per day from whole grains, legumes, fruits and vegetables.
Total Sugars Watch
This includes both natural sugars (from fruit, dairy) and added sugars (from processing). Canada's label doesn't separate them - check the ingredients list for clues. Words ending in "-ose" (glucose, fructose, sucrose) and syrups are added sugars. Limit added sugars to less than 10% of daily calories.
Protein Good
Protein builds and repairs tissues, makes enzymes, and keeps you feeling full. No % DV is shown in Canada because protein deficiency is rare. Adults need roughly 0.8g per kg of body weight daily. Prioritize plant-based proteins (legumes, tofu, nuts) alongside poultry, fish, and eggs.
